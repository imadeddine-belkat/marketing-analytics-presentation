The Archives

Evolution of the Field
1992

Tapestry System

Goldberg et al. introduce the term 'collaborative filtering' in a system that used human feedback to filter information for other users.

2001

Item-Based CF

Sarwar et al. propose item-based collaborative filtering, comparing items rather than users. Important for e-commerce because product catalogs are more stable than user behavior.

2003

Amazon's Approach

Linden et al. describe Amazon's item-to-item CF model, explaining why comparing items was much faster and produced more consistent results than comparing users.

2009

Matrix Factorization

Koren et al. win the Netflix Prize using matrix factorization, representing users and items as vectors of latent factors and predicting preferences by multiplying them.

2015

Full Hybrid Architecture

Gomez-Uribe & Hunt describe Netflix's full recommendation architecture, combining content ranking, row ordering, and personalized thumbnail selection.

2019

Deep Learning Era

Zhang et al. survey deep learning methods for recommendation, showing how neural networks learn complex patterns that simpler models cannot capture.

2023

LLM-Based Systems

Wu et al. review three LLM approaches: generating feature embeddings, producing recommendations directly, and acting as conversational agents to understand preferences.

The Ingredients

Core Data Structures

User Data

History of purchases, clicks, ratings, and time spent on pages. The behavioral blueprint that reveals preference patterns.

Item Data

Product features such as category, price, brand, and description. The structural anatomy of the catalog.

Context Data

Time of day, device used, location, and current session activity. The environmental variables shaping each interaction.

Performance Specifications

How Performance Is Measured

Offline Metrics (Accuracy)

Precision@K

Out of the top K recommended items, how many did the user actually interact with?

Recall@K

Out of all items the user liked, how many appeared in the top K recommendations?

NDCG

Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain. Measures whether the best items appear at the top of the list.

Online Metrics (Business)

Click-Through Rate (CTR)

How often users click on a recommended item.

Conversion Rate

How often a click leads to a purchase.

AOV & CLV

Whether recommendations increase the amount spent per order (AOV) and long-term customer loyalty (CLV).

The Signature Collection

Primary Filtering Models

Collaborative Filtering

Finds users with similar behavior and recommends what they liked. Does not need product information. Includes User-Based CF, Item-Based CF, and Matrix Factorization.

Foundation

Content-Based

Matches items to a user profile built from item features (TF-IDF, Word2Vec, BERT). Especially useful for new items that have no interaction history yet.

Precision

The Hybrid Ensemble

Combines collaborative and content-based methods to reduce their individual weaknesses. Strategies include Weighted, Switching, and Cascade approaches. Used by Netflix.

Advanced

Knowledge-Based

Uses rules and user requirements to filter items. Good for expensive or complex products. Requires a detailed knowledge base to be built first.

Specialty
The Haute Couture of RecSys

Deep Learning Suite

Neural networks that learn complex, non-linear patterns in user behavior that simpler models cannot capture.

  • Neural CF (NCF) Replaces dot product with neural net
  • Wide & Deep Memorization + generalization (Google Play)
  • Sequential (SASRec) Attention over interaction sequences
  • Two-Tower Models Fast nearest-neighbor search at serving
  • LLM-Based Text-driven, useful when behavioral data is limited

Case 01 / Retail

Amazon

Item-Based Collaborative Filtering.

With tens of millions of users, calculating similarities between all pairs of users was too slow. Item-item relationships are more stable and faster to compute. This engineering trade-off sacrifices some user-specific flexibility but dramatically improves scalability and latency. Reported to generate around 35% of total revenue.

Case 03 / Audio

Spotify

Context-Aware Recommendation.

Discover Weekly creates 30 personalized songs every Monday by combining three signal types: collaborative filtering based on playlist co-occurrence patterns, audio analysis of track features such as tempo, energy, and mood, and NLP on playlist names and user-generated descriptions. Context also plays a role, learning that users prefer high-energy music during commutes and slower music in the evening.

Care & Handling

System Limitations & Analysis

When a new user joins a platform, the system has no behavioral data about them. Similarly, when a new product is added, there is no co-purchase or co-view history to work with. Recommendation quality is poor precisely when a good first impression matters most. Hybrid systems that use content-based filtering as a fallback help reduce this problem, and some platforms ask new users to rate a few items during onboarding to collect initial preference data.

Recommendation systems tend to show users more of what they have already shown interest in. Over time, this can narrow what users discover. Some platforms try to address this by deliberately including diverse or unexpected items — introducing 'serendipity' into the system. However, there is a trade-off: too much diversity reduces relevance and can frustrate users.

Recommendation systems depend on collecting large amounts of user data. This creates privacy concerns, especially under regulations like the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which requires transparency about data use and the right to deletion. There is also a fairness concern: if historical data reflects biases, the recommendation model will learn and repeat them. Addressing algorithmic fairness is an active area of research (Tintarev, 2022).

Maison des Algorithmes

Recommendation systems have become a core part of e-commerce marketing, enabling personalized experiences at scale. The field has evolved from simple memory-based collaborative filtering in the 1990s to complex hybrid deep learning systems today, and is now entering a new phase with the integration of large language models. Evidence from Amazon, Netflix, and Spotify shows that well-designed systems directly increase revenue, reduce churn, and improve customer satisfaction. As these systems become more powerful, responsible design, with transparency, fairness, and privacy safeguards, becomes increasingly paramount.
